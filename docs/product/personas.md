# Frontend Realms — Player Personas

## Persona 1: Maya Chen — The Junior Developer Ready to Level Up

### Background

Maya is 24, 18 months out of a coding bootcamp, and works as a junior frontend developer at a mid-size SaaS company. She writes React components daily — mostly forms, tables, and data display — but feels insecure whenever the conversation moves beyond basic rendering. She has never written a server component. She does not know what a hydration mismatch looks like. She uses `useEffect` for everything and has never thought about caching strategies.

Her team uses Next.js, but Maya only touches pages already set up by senior engineers. She avoids the routing config, the data-fetching layer, and anything involving the `app/` directory structure.

### Goals

- Build confidence in modern React and Next.js patterns
- Understand server components well enough to use them deliberately (not just copy from examples)
- Fix bugs without asking for help every time
- Earn a promotion to mid-level within the next 12 months
- Stop feeling like an imposter in architecture discussions

### Frustrations

- Tutorials are too simple ("click here, type this") and don't prepare her for real bugs
- Courses assume she already knows what she is trying to learn
- Codecademy felt like it held her hand too much and never taught her to debug
- LeetCode is irrelevant to her job
- She doesn't know what she doesn't know — and there is no tool that shows her gaps clearly
- Senior engineers are too busy to mentor her systematically

### How Maya Uses the Platform

- **First visit**: Takes the diagnostic, which correctly identifies that she understands basic React (JSX, props, simple state) but has no Server Component mental model and weak async JavaScript fundamentals. The diagnostic places her at the border of The JavaScript Foundations and The React Reactor.
- **Daily sessions**: 25–35 minutes, usually after lunch when her focus dips. She works through Knowledge Encounters and Code Forensics missions to strengthen her async fundamentals.
- **Weekly sessions**: Weekend 60–90 minute sessions where she takes on Bug Hunts and Build Quests. She builds a typed search interface as part of a multi-session Build Quest.
- **When stuck**: She uses progressive hints (typically 1 or 2) rather than brute-forcing. She knows that hints reduce immediate rewards, but learning is the point.
- **After 3 months**: She has unlocked The Next.js Nexus, understands Server/Client boundaries, and has defeated the Hydration Hydra boss. Her confidence is visibly higher.

### What Success Looks Like

Maya leads the migration of her team's legacy pages from Pages Router to App Router. She identifies a hydration mismatch during code review and explains the fix to a peer. She no longer avoids architecture discussions. She gets the mid-level title and starts eyeing senior.

## Persona 2: James Okafor — The Mid-Level Engineer Preparing for Senior

### Background

James is 31, with 6 years of frontend experience. He has worked at two startups and one larger tech company, doing React, some Angular, and recently full-time Next.js. He is technically competent — he ships features, knows the framework, and has built real products. But his senior interviews keep falling short. Twice now, interviewers have told him he needs deeper understanding of rendering strategies, caching, performance profiling, and architectural trade-offs. He can build it, but he cannot explain _why_ one approach is better than another.

James has a family and limited study time. He needs efficiency.

### Goals

- Fill the specific gaps that have cost him senior offers
- Develop the vocabulary and structured reasoning to discuss trade-offs fluently
- Practice system design and architecture under interview-like conditions
- Earn a senior frontend engineer title within 6 months
- Build a portfolio of practical understanding, not just interview trivia

### Frustrations

- General interview prep platforms either test algorithms (irrelevant) or behavioural questions (superficial)
- Books and documentation teach _what_, not _why_ or _when to choose differently_
- He cannot afford a mock-interview coach
- He has already read the docs — he needs to _apply_ and _reason_
- His study time is limited to 4–5 hours a week; every minute must count

### How James Uses the Platform

- **First visit**: Skips most of the diagnostic's JavaScript and React foundation questions (already strong). The system adapts and pushes him into Architecture Council and Production Incident missions within the first session.
- **Daily sessions**: Short 15-minute Interview Arena missions during his commute — explaining concepts, answering follow-up questions, doing timed architecture discussions.
- **Weekly sessions**: 2–3 hour deep-dive on weekends. Boss battles (The Rendering Architect, The Cache Phantom), Production Incidents, and Architecture Council multi-part missions.
- **Focus**: He manually selects mission types that target his gap areas — caching, rendering strategy, data fetching patterns.
- **Spaced review**: The system flags his knowledge of `stale-while-revalidate` is decaying after 10 days without review. James does a quick Code Forensics mission to reinforce it.

### What Success Looks Like

James gets the senior offer. During the interview, when asked "how would you design the data layer for a real-time dashboard?", he walks through Server Components, streaming, optimistic updates, cache invalidation, error boundaries, and fallback states — with trade-offs for each approach. The interviewer is impressed. James credits Frontend Realms for giving him both the knowledge and the structured way to express it.

## Persona 3: Priya Kapoor — The Career-Switcher Needing Structure

### Background

Priya is 29, with 4 years of backend Java experience at a large enterprise. She decided to transition to frontend because she enjoys UI work and sees more growth opportunities. She understands programming deeply (data structures, networking, databases) but knows almost nothing about the browser, React, CSS, or frontend architecture. She tried a few online courses, but they moved too slowly on concepts she already knew from backend (like async, error handling, design patterns) and too quickly on concepts that were new (like the DOM, reconciliation, the event loop).

She needs a path that adapts to what she already knows and what she does not.

### Goals

- Build frontend competence without starting from zero
- Get hired as a mid-level frontend developer (she cannot afford a junior salary cut)
- Understand the modern Next.js ecosystem well enough to pass technical interviews
- Build a portfolio of real frontend work
- Close the gap in 6–9 months while continuing her current job

### Frustrations

- Most frontend courses assume zero programming experience — they bore her with basic loops and variables
- No platform adapts to her existing backend knowledge
- She struggles with CSS and layout — a domain with completely different mental models from backend
- She does not know which gaps matter most for hiring
- The frontend ecosystem changes too fast — she needs a living curriculum, not a 2021 course

### How Priya Uses the Platform

- **First visit**: The diagnostic quickly detects she is strong on programming concepts (variables, functions, error handling, async logic) but weak on browser APIs, DOM, CSS, component lifecycle, and React mental models. It skips JavaScript fundamentals and places her directly in The JavaScript Foundations (advanced section: closures, prototypes, modules) and The React Reactor.
- **Daily sessions**: 20–30 minutes, focused on areas where she is weakest. She does a mix of Knowledge Encounters for new concepts and Code Forensics to see how patterns work in practice.
- **Weekly sessions**: Build Quests — she builds progressively more complex features. First a simple product card, then a server-rendered product page with search, then authentication, then a real-time dashboard.
- **Side focus**: She spends intentional time in The Network Depths and The Performance Wastes because those cross-cut her backend knowledge.
- **Cross-training**: The system periodically serves her missions that connect frontend concepts to her backend knowledge — e.g., comparing REST vs GraphQL vs Server Functions from both perspectives.

### What Success Looks Like

Priya lands a mid-level frontend role at a tech company within 8 months. Her new team is surprised by her strong architectural reasoning (from backend) and her rapidly improving component design and CSS skills. She continues using the platform to fill gaps in testing, accessibility, and production debugging as she grows into the role.

## Persona 4: Dr. Elena Vasquez — The Senior Engineer Filling Gaps

### Background

Elena is 38, a principal frontend engineer at a fintech company with 12+ years of experience. She has deep expertise in Angular, Vanilla JavaScript, performance, and accessibility. But her company is migrating to Next.js, and she needs to ramp up quickly — not just learn the syntax but truly understand the architecture, caching model, and server component paradigm well enough to lead the migration and make sound architectural decisions.

She also mentors junior engineers and wants to understand common pitfalls so she can guide her team effectively. She does not need a structured curriculum — she needs targeted deep-dives into specific Next.js areas, with the ability to skip what she already knows.

### Goals

- Master Next.js App Router internals faster than her team needs her to
- Understand the server component execution model (not just usage)
- Evaluate caching strategies for her company's data patterns
- Identify migration risks before they become production incidents
- Build internal training materials informed by her play-through

### Frustrations

- Existing Next.js courses are too basic for her level
- She does not have time for a 40-hour course when she needs specific answers now
- Documentation tells her what APIs exist but does not help her develop judgment about when to use which
- Peer learning is slow; she learns faster by being challenged
- Most interview-prep and skill-assessment platforms are targeted at juniors and insult her experience

### How Elena Uses the Platform

- **First visit**: The diagnostic tries to test her, but she answers most JavaScript, React, and testing questions correctly. The system quickly adapts and pushes into advanced territory — Production Incidents, Architecture Council, and Boss Battles in The Next.js Nexus and The Rendering Frontier.
- **Session pattern**: Irregular but intense. She might spend 2 hours on a Saturday going through a chain of Production Incident simulations, then not open the app for 4 days. The streak system's grace days accommodate this without penalty.
- **Mission selection**: She uses the skill-map view to directly target concepts she wants to explore: ISR vs ODR, stale-while-revalidate semantics, Partial Prerendering trade-offs, and server-component composition patterns.
- **Evaluation mode**: She often uses Explanation challenges to articulate her understanding, which the system evaluates for clarity and completeness. This helps her solidify mental models.
- **Interview Arena**: She uses it to prepare for conference talks and internal tech discussions, practising how to explain complex Next.js concepts simply.

### What Success Looks Like

Elena leads her company's successful migration to Next.js App Router with zero production incidents. She uses the knowledge maps to identify the five highest-risk migration areas and dedicates extra testing there. She internally runs a "Next.js Architecture" workshop that she developed partly from her platform play-through. She recommends Frontend Realms to three mid-level engineers on her team, each of whom starts their own journey.
